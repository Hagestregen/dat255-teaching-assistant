## Improving generalisation (2:42)

When starting deep learning projects or any machine learning endeavor, we must acknowledge that we do not have infinite amounts of training data. We hope to have a lot, but we can always seek more. Training data may not fully capture all possibilities for what something can look like. For instance, if classifying trains and lacking pictures of all possible trains, we would like our methods to exhibit some degree of generalizability, recognizing other objects with wheels on railroad tracks that share similar properties, even if they are not specifically trained examples.

To address this, one simple yet effective trick involves modifying the examples we have artificially. This can be done in a way that preserves the underlying patterns and information. By introducing randomness, we can generate multiple modified versions of our existing data. These modified versions can then be used alongside the original data for training.

For example, if we have a single picture of a cat, we can modify it slightly—rotate it, flip it, or even completely invert it. While these modifications might alter the visual appearance, they should still convey the essential features of a cat. This approach allows us to expand the space of realistic images our models can learn from. By doing this, we effectively multiply our training data set, enhancing the diversity and robustness of our machine learning methods.

## Augmentation (6:03)

So just call it augmentation in general. You can do this for any type of data, particularly for computer vision, where it's obvious how to apply it since we can easily visualize the results. If you have other types of data, such as sound or voice classification, you can also modify this approach and still listen to the audio. For data that doesn't have a clear visual interpretation, like tables of data, it can be more challenging to determine how much you can tweak the data without breaking the correlations we've established. However, for images, we can simply check if they still make sense after modification. Tweaking images is straightforward; we can do it in various ways, such as flipping, rotating, cropping, adjusting brightness, changing colors, and applying equalization. Each time an image is processed, it undergoes a random transformation, such as flipping or rotating, to preserve the information while maintaining the image's integrity.

For training, we set the augmentation layer to `training=False` during inference to ensure the final predictions are as accurate as possible. During training, we enable the augmentation layer to apply transformations like flipping, rotating, cropping, adjusting brightness, and changing colors. This helps in generating more diverse training data, which can improve the model's performance.

For example, consider a dog image. Without any augmentation, it remains a dog. By flipping it, we create a mirrored version, which is still a dog. Rotating it slightly or cropping parts of it while ensuring it remains recognizable as a dog is also feasible. Adjusting brightness or changing colors slightly can help maintain the dog-like appearance. To preserve the image's dimensions, we can mirror the parts that fall outside the original boundaries.

There are several built-in augmentation methods available, such as `RandomFlip`, `RandomRotation`, `RandomCrop`, `RandomBrightness`, `RandomColorJitter`, and `Equalize`. These methods can be combined to achieve a wide range of transformations. For instance, we might apply a combination of flipping and saturation to generate a variety of augmented images.

In terms of effectiveness, studies show that adding augmentation significantly improves model performance by providing more diverse training data. The choice of which transformations to apply depends on the specific task and dataset. For example, if classifying images of objects with varying orientations, we wouldn't want to include random rotations that could misclassify these images.

To evaluate the effectiveness of augmentation, you can experiment with different types of augmentations and compare the test set accuracy before and after applying augmentation. This can help you understand the impact of augmentation on model performance.

## Residual connections (35:36)

Networks, but there is one nice trick that is now used for both computer vision and other types of models: residual connections. I will show what this is and what it solves.

First, let’s think back. Once we have deep learning, we have stacked layers, performing composite functions in a sense. Each layer transforms data based on the transformation of the previous layers. This can go wrong in many ways, such as the numbers growing too large, causing gradient descent issues, or becoming too small, leading to optimization problems. A good analogy is having a microphone next to a speaker; the signal can amplify or diminish in various ways.

To address these issues, we can introduce residual connections. The idea is to reintroduce signal at different points in the model, preventing the numbers from exploding or disappearing. This can be achieved by copying the input and adding it to the output of each layer.

The trick is simple but requires branching and combining arrows at different points. These are called residual connections or skip connections. Essentially, for each input, we pass it through the layer and also copy it, adding it to the output. This ensures that information flows through the network while maintaining the original input.

This approach helps prevent the gradients from exploding or vanishing, making it easier to optimize the model. For very deep networks, adding more layers doesn’t necessarily improve performance; in fact, it can lead to degradation.
