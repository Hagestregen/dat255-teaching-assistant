## Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (34:27)

Tomorrow I’ll repeat this, but let’s look at convolutions and then digest the concept. Convolutions involve making computations with predefined filters learned from training data. However, this only gives us knowledge about the training data; we don’t retain any information from new data. For instance, if we predict stock prices in 2026 using convolutions based on data from 2023, we won’t remember the specifics of 2023. To address this, we need to add a state to our model, representing memory. This allows the model to remember patterns seen and use them for predictions beyond the training data. 

This is known as recurrence, and a recurrent neural network (RNN) is an example. Recurrence involves patterns repeating, and the simplest approach is to store the output from one time step and reintroduce it for the next. This helps improve predictions by incorporating past information. 

However, this introduces a loop, making computations more complex. We can unroll this process in time, simulating the loop. The first step is straightforward: inputs are processed, and outputs are generated. For subsequent steps, inputs are processed alongside the stored state, propagating through time. 

The concept isn’t difficult but requires understanding how to manage the state during computation. During training, the model must remain differentiable, and the state must be updated accordingly. Training RNNs typically takes longer than non-recurrent models due to the need to handle inputs in the correct order and store/retrieve states. 

For certain types of RNNs, specialized implementations exist that speed up computations. However, custom implementations may require more time. Our focus now is learning how RNNs work.
